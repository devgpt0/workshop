# Sakshi and the Festival of Lamps

## Chunk 01

In the old city of Varanasi, where temple bells greeted every sunrise, lived a kind girl named Sakshi. After her father passed away, Sakshi was left in the care of her stepmother and two stepsisters. They wore bright silks and gold bangles, while Sakshi was given faded cotton and endless chores. She cleaned brass lamps, fetched water from the ghat, and cooked meals she was not allowed to eat first. Even then, Sakshi kept her gentle smile and helped neighbors whenever she could.

## Chunk 02

As the great Diwali festival approached, the royal palace announced a grand celebration for all families in the kingdom. The prince, Aarav, wished to meet someone who understood both compassion and courage. Sakshi longed to see the palace lights just once, but her stepmother laughed and piled extra work before her. When the household left for the celebration, Sakshi sat alone in the courtyard beside an unlit diya. An elderly woman appeared near the tulsi plant, dressed like a wandering storyteller.

## Chunk 03

She had kind eyes and said she had watched Sakshi share food with hungry children. With a soft prayer, she touched Sakshi's clay lamp and it blazed with warm golden light. Sakshi's simple clothes transformed into a graceful lehenga the color of marigold petals, and glass slippers became embroidered juttis. A bullock cart turned into a sparkling carriage decorated with jasmine strings. The woman warned Sakshi: the magic would fade when the temple clock struck midnight. At the palace, rows of diyas shimmered like stars fallen to earth.

## Chunk 04

Prince Aarav noticed Sakshi not because of her clothes, but because she bowed respectfully to the palace cooks and thanked the guards. They spoke about books, rivers, and the duty of serving people before power. For the first time in years, Sakshi felt seen for who she truly was. As drums thundered and dancers spun, the first midnight bell rang. Sakshi remembered the warning and ran down the marble steps. In her hurry, one embroidered jutti slipped off and remained on the palace staircase.

## Chunk 05

By the final bell, the carriage was gone, the lehenga had changed back, and Sakshi stood again in her old cotton dress. The next morning, Prince Aarav announced he would search for the person whose kindness had moved him. He carried the single jutti through every neighborhood, asking each household to let someone try it. At Sakshi's home, her stepsisters pushed forward first, but the jutti would not fit either of them.

## Chunk 06

Sakshi's stepmother tried to hide her in the kitchen, yet Aarav heard a familiar voice humming a prayer song. When Sakshi stepped out, the jutti fit perfectly. Aarav asked not for proof of wealth, but one question: would she help him build a fair kingdom where no child slept hungry. Sakshi said yes with steady confidence. Soon they were married in a ceremony lit by thousands of diyas, with music from shehnai and laughter from the whole city. Sakshi invited her stepfamily too, choosing compassion over revenge.

## Chunk 07

As queen, she opened schools for girls, community kitchens for workers, and shelters for widows and travelers. People remembered her not as a girl rescued by magic, but as a woman who transformed kindness into leadership. And every Diwali, she lit the first lamp herself, reminding everyone that even the smallest flame can change a life.
